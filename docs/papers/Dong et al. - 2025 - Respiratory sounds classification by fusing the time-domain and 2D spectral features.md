Most existing methods only extract features from two-dimensional time-frequency representations, which capture rich frequency-domain information but often overlook critical time-domain features of audio signals. To address this limitation, we propose a novel classification method that integrates both time-domain features and two-dimensional time-frequency features of respiratory sounds. Specifically, a Mel encoder is employed to extract spectral features from the Mel spectrum, while a time-domain feature extraction module captures the time-domain characteristics of the original audio signal. ## 1. Introduction

### 1.1. Motivation

Respiratory diseases are substantial public health issue, affecting a large number of people worldwide [1]. Specifically, when patients suffer from respiratory diseases, certain changes occur in the airways or pulmonary alveolus that result in various adventitious sounds superimposed on the respiratory sounds [2]. Doctors generally diagnose abnormal respiratory sounds through pulmonary auscultation by placing the chestpiece of a stethoscope on the patient’s chest or back. However, there are some limitations to using the stethoscope for diagnosis [3]. Firstly, because auscultation requires professional doctors and continuous monitoring over a long period of time, it demands a significant amount of manpower and medical resources, which poses a challenge to widespread application in real world. Secondly, the results of auscultation are heavily influenced by the subjectivity of the doctor, and different doctors may have discrepancies even when listening to the same lung sounds. Finally, as a qualitative analysis method, it is difficult to quantify abnormal respiratory sounds by comparing the diagnostic results of different individuals. ∗ Corresponding authors. In contrast, respiratory sounds classification (RSC) involves the use of machine learning models to automatically classify features of respiratory sounds, which can complete classification tasks more efficiently. Therefore, the development of effective RSC algorithms holds considerable practical importance and application value, as it significantly enhances medical quality and patient life quality. Through accurately classification of respiratory sounds, doctors can rapidly diagnose different respiratory patterns, which may lead to the early detection of diseases like pneumonia, asthma, and chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) [4]. Moreover, these algorithms can provide personalized treatment plans for patients, facilitate precision medicine, and offer effective screening tools for public health events [5]. In recent years, researchers have devised a range of algorithms aimed at improving the Score of RSC [6–9].

Ultimately, deep learning networks such as ResNet [10–15], ResNeSt [16], Temporal Convolutional Networks (TCN) [17], Swin Transformer [18], Hierarchical Token-Semantic Audio Transformer (HTS-AT) [19] and Audio Spectrogram Transformer (AST) [20–24] are utilized for feature extraction and classification. In the last decades, researchers attempted to design a series of RSC algorithms, most of which employ deep learning models. Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) is widely used due to its excellent performance in feature extraction and handling complex data. For example, S. Although the RSC algorithm based on CNN achieved considerable success, the inherent limitations of convolution operations impeded the model’s ability to capture long-range global context. Recently, the advent of the Transformer model compensated for this limitation, thereby encouraging more researchers to adopt the Transformer architecture. For example, W. Significantly, the MVST extracts features a total of five times and finally employs a Gated Fusion for feature integration, which consequently achieves an accuracy Score of 66.55% on the ICBHI 2017 dataset. Meanwhile, self-supervised speech pre-training models based on Transformer have demonstrated effectiveness in the extraction of timedomain features for Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) [11,26–31]. For instance, models of the Wav2vec family [27–29] enhanced recognition accuracy by learning to represent time-domain features through predicting masked portions of audio segments. What is more, the Hubert model achieved time-domain feature extraction by means of discretization, masked prediction, and iterative refinement for cluster assignments [30]. Given the successful application of self-supervised speech pre-training methods in ASR, it is potential that applies these techniques to augment 2D time-frequency representations, especially since this approach has not been explored in RSC.
### 1.3. Contribution

The above works mainly involve extracting features solely from 2D time-frequency representations, but this is inadequate. Although Spec or Mel-Spec offers extensive information regarding the frequencydomain characteristics of respiratory sound signals, these methods generally overlook the time-domain properties of audio signals [32]. Diagnosing complex respiratory diseases often requires a comprehensive analysis of respiratory sounds from multiple perspectives. Time domain information is focused on how the audio signal changes over time. And it retains subtle changes in respiration. Therefore, extracting the time-domain information of the audio signal is necessary for better understanding of respiratory sounds. Additionally, a key challenge is how to integrate the time-domain features with 2D time-frequency representations, enabling the model to learn these combined features efficiently. Hence, in this study, we propose a method that combines timedomain and 2D spectral features for RSC: (1) We design the Extract Time-Domain Features and Extract 2D Time-Frequency Features module separately, to extract timedomain and 2D spectral features, respectively, where we use a self-supervised speech pre-training module to extract timedomain features. (2) We investigate an effective co-attention fusion strategy that combines the time-domain features with 2D time-frequency representations, enabling the model to comprehensively understand the respiratory sounds from multiple perspectives. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to fuse two acoustic features for RSC. (3) On the ICBHI 2017 and SPRSound datasets, our method achieves commendable performance. Specifically, our results on the ICBHI 2017 dataset exceed the previous state-of-the-art (sota) by 1.00%. Moreover, on the SPRSound dataset, in task1-1, task1- 2, task2-1 and task2-2, we surpass the previous best result by 3.01%, 4.89%, 5.70% and 3.87%, respectively. These results corroborate that our proposed method not only demonstrates substantial superiority in the task of RSC, but also showcases excellent robustness. In Section 3, the experiments are introduced, including Datasets, Evaluation Metrics, Experimental Details, as well as Comparison with sota methods and Ablation Experiments. Moreover, in Section 4, we discuss the advantages and limitations of our proposed framework. Finally, in Section 5, some conclusion of the entire paper is provided.

### 2.2. Pre-processing

Before inputting the audio signal into the model, we perform the following pre-processing measures. Considering that the respiratory sound data in the original data set was collected from different devices with sampling rates ranging from 4 kHz to 44.1 kHz, we resampled all audio to 16 kHz. Higher sampling rates should increase storage costs and computing power requirements. Conversely, lower sampling rates should cause loss of relevant information. In the ICBHI dataset, the length of breathing cycles ranges from 0.2 s to 16.2 s. This poses a problem while training our network as it expects a fixed size input. We experimented with different input lengths, and found an 8 s length to perform best. Shorter durations (e.g., 4 s) lead to cropping of samples and thus loss of valuable information in an already scarce dataset, while longer durations (e.g., 16 s) may introduce redundant information and consequently reduce model performance. For audio clips shorter than 8 s, we apply circular padding to maintain the required length, whereas longer clips are appropriately truncated. Finally, considering the variations in sound intensity across different recordings, we perform amplitude normalization to ensure consistent input scales. To enhance the robustness of time-domain features, we use Vocal Tract Length Perturbation Patch Augmentation(VTLP-Patch Aug) to process audio. Finally, we use the obtained 𝑥𝑤 for time domain feature extraction and 2D time-frequency feature extraction. ### 2.3. Extract time-domain features

Previous RSC algorithms mainly relied on Mel-Spec to reflect the 2D frequency-domain information of audio, but paid less attention to time-domain features. So we complement an additional module that is specifically designed for extracting time-domain features. The orange box in Fig. 1 illustrates the process of extracting time-domain features. After VTLP-Patch Aug, the features in the time-domain are extracted from 𝑥𝑤(𝑡). The time-domain features extraction model comprises a multi-layered convolutional encoder, a Transformer for context representation, and a quantization module. The time-domain features encoder consists of multiple convolutional layers specifically designed to extract useful time features directly from 𝑥𝑤(𝑡). Following every convolutional layer, the GELU activation function and Layer Normalization (LN) are implemented to improve the model’s nonlinear processing ability and ensure stability during training. Importantly, the use of a stride exceeding 1 for downsampling significantly reduces the dimensionality of the data and computational demands. Additionally, the encoder for time features utilizes multi-scale convolutional kernels to extract diverse time-scale characteristics present in the audio, providing the contextual Transformer network with diverse and hierarchical features. ### 2.4. Extract 2D time-frequency features

We first transform the 𝑥𝑤 into a Mel-Spec (𝑥𝑠 ) and then pass the 𝑥𝑠 through Mel-encoder to obtain a two-dimensional time-frequency features (𝑥 ′ 𝑠 ). For obtaining Mel-Spec, the detailed steps are as follows: 𝑥𝑤 is segmented into shorter frames, with each frame set to a length of 30 ms and a frame shift of 15 ms. Subsequently, the Hamming window is added to each frame to minimize spectral leakage and guarantee smooth transitions to zero at the start and end of each frame. Furthermore, Fast Fourier Transformation (FFT) is applied to each frame to convert the time-domain signals into the frequency-domain, thereby obtaining the spectrum of each frame. At last, we convert the frequency-domain signals to a sequence of 128-dimensional log Mel filterbank (Fbank) features and perform normalization to obtain 𝑥𝑠 . ### 3.1. Datasets

At the event level, the numbers of ‘‘Normal’’, ‘‘Rhonchi’’, ‘‘Wheeze’’, ‘‘Stridor’’, ‘‘Coarse Crackle’’, ‘‘Fine Crackle’’, and ‘‘Wheeze & Crackle’’ are 2458, 95, 194, 32, 34, 309, and 2, respectively. The mean duration of respiratory sound events and records are 1.3 s and 11 s, respectively. This dataset is divided into two tasks for classification from respiratory sound events (task1), and respiratory sound recordings (task2). In task1, task1-1 is binary classification of Normal and Abnormal respiratory sound events and task1-2 is multiple classification of Normal, Rhonchi, Wheeze, Stridor, Coarse Crackle, Fine Crackle, and Wheeze & Crackle respiratory sound events. In task 2, task 2-1 is a triple classification of normal, abnormal and poor quality respiratory sound recordings, and task 2-2 is a multi-class classification of Normal, CAS, DAS, CAS & DAS, and Poor Quality respiratory sound recordings. It should be mentioned that since the two data sets are not labeled in the same way and have different classification tasks, we trained and tested for each set individually.

## 4. Discussion

Our method demonstrates that extracting features from both the time domain and 2D time-frequency representations significantly improves RSC performance. (1) Firstly, time domain features incorporate important details like waveform variations and the correlation between sequences. By learning time domain features, the model can identify critical characteristics such as trends and periodicity in audio. Additionally, considering the correlation between elements in a sequence can help improve prediction accuracy. (2) Furthermore, 2D time-frequency representations focus more on the relationship between the time and frequency domain. Specifically, dividing the Mel-Spec into the 16 × 16 size aims to balance the time-frequency representations within each patch. Thus, as shown in Table 4, whether it is our method or MVST, the 16 × 16 size consistently achieves better Score. (3) In contrast to simple concatenation, co-attention effectively integrates the extracted time-frequency features, enabling the model to learn the complex relationships between them. This fusion strategy contributes to improving the accuracy and robustness of classification. (4) Unlike data augmentation on the Mel-Spec, VTLP-Patch Aug introduces variability directly into the original audio signal, which helps reduce overfitting issues caused by a limited number of samples and enhances the robustness against environmental noise. Table 5 demonstrates the effectiveness of co-attention and VTLP-Patch Aug, with these modules improving Score by 2.34% and 3.19%, respectively. Although our method showed good results, it has several limitations. First, our study was based on public ICBHI 2017 dataset and SPRSound dataset and has not yet been tested in real-world clinical data. Although ICBHI 2017 dataset includes recordings from diverse devices, generalizability to more heterogeneous devices in real-world settings remains a limitation. Future work could expand training data to include a wider range of device types. We will also explore domain adaptation or transfer learning to enhance adaptability to varying recording conditions. Second, the real-world recordings often contain ambient noise. We addressed this partially through VTLP-Patch augmentation, which simulates noise perturbations. However, incorporating real-world noise datasets or advanced noise-robust techniques could further improve robustness. Third, class imbalance is also a common challenge in respiratory sound classification. The model demonstrates Biomedical Signal Processing and Control 107 (2025) 107790 8 G. Dong et al. poor recognition ability when faced with a small number of samples (such as Wheeze & Crackle). We mitigated this by using data augmentation and balanced training batches, but real-world data may exhibit more pronounced imbalance, warranting further attention. Fourth, to ensure a fair comparison with existing models, we used the Score as the main evaluation metric. However, in specific clinical problems, the evaluation indicators need to be selected according to specific requirements. For instance, in the case of large-scale early-stage screening, 𝑆𝑒 takes precedence. When it comes to the diagnosis process, 𝑆𝑝 is more important. In the future, we will explore and discuss the 𝑆𝑒 -𝑆𝑝 trade-off in greater depth. Finally, we use a self-supervised speech pretraining module to extract time-domain features in this study. Due to the differences between respiratory sounds and speech, the model may not precisely capture the subtle nuances of respiratory sounds, preventing our method from achieving optimal performance. In the future, we will collect more unlabeled respiratory audio datasets to train a self-supervised respiratory sound pre-training model for the time-domain features extraction.
## 5. Conclusion

In this paper, we propose a RSC method that extracts time-domain and 2D time-frequency representations features separately, and then fuses them using a co-attention module before feeding them into a classifier. Instead of simply concatenating time-domain and 2D spectral features, we develop a co-attention module to effectively fuse both the time-domain and the spectral representations. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to perform RSC by fusing twolayer acoustic features. As expected, this innovative operation improves classification performance, experimental results on the ICBHI 2017 dataset and SPRSound dataset demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. In future work, we will extend the time-domain and 2D spectral features fusion approach to the event detection of respiratory sounds.
