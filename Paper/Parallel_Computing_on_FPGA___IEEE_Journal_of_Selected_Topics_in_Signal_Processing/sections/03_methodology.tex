\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

This study proposes a resource-efficient, cascaded diagnostic framework designed to address the inherent trade-off between computational complexity and classification accuracy in edge-based respiratory analysis. The methodology is structured into three primary stages: acoustic signal processing, cascaded inference logic, and hardware-aware implementation.

\subsection{Dataset and Preprocessing}
To construct a more comprehensive and robust evaluation benchmark, the proposed framework is evaluated using a combined respiratory sound database totaling 1,256 audio recordings. This unified corpus integrates two distinct data sources to maximize demographic and clinical diversity:

\textit{1) ICBHI Database:} The International Conference on Biomedical and Health Informatics (ICBHI) 2017 respiratory sound database \cite{b16} is leveraged as the primary public benchmark. This subset encompasses 920 recordings, comprising 6,898 respiratory cycles from 126 subjects with varied pathologies (e.g., COPD, Asthma, Pneumonia, URTI, Bronchiectasis) alongside healthy controls. Ranging from 5 to 90 seconds in duration, these signals were captured across seven distinct chest locations (trachea, anterior, posterior, and lateral) utilizing four different stethoscope models in both clinical and non-clinical environments.

\textit{2) King Abdullah University Hospital Database:} To supplement the core data, we integrate a secondary clinical database originating from the King Abdullah University Hospital project (Jordan University of Science and Technology) \cite{kauh_db}. This subset contributes recordings from an additional 112 subjects (77 presenting with diverse respiratory diseases and 35 healthy subjects). Sourced via a 3M Littmann 3200 Electronic Stethoscope, each recording spans 5 to 30 seconds to capture at least one complete respiratory cycle from a standard anterior or posterior chest location. For diagnostic clarity, subjects explicitly diagnosed with more than two concurrent diseases were systematically excluded from this analysis.

To establish a standardized classification task, all recordings from both sources (treating each recording as one independent sample) are mapped into a unified 3-class target space: COPD (820 samples, 65.3\%), Non-COPD (296 samples, 23.6\%), and Healthy (140 samples, 11.1\%). The generalized Non-COPD class aggregates distinct pathologies such as Asthma, Heart Failure, Pulmonary Fibrosis, Pneumonia, and Pleural Effusion among others. As detailed in Table \ref{tab:class_dist}, the combined class distribution remains profoundly imbalanced, necessitating robust feature discrimination and ensemble-driven strategies.

Prior to feature extraction, raw audio signals undergo a preprocessing pipeline including resampling to 4kHz and band-pass filtering to isolate pulmonary acoustic bands, effectively mitigating ambient noise. For the statistical feature screening layers (Layers 1--3), a band-pass filter of 50Hz - 2500Hz is applied along with 5-second signal segmentation. For the deep learning layer (Layer 4), a band-pass filter of 25Hz - 2000Hz is utilized with an 8-second segment to maximize spectral resolution for spatial-spectral representations.

\subsection{Feature Extraction Engineering}
To capture the non-stationary nature of respiratory signals (e.g., transient crackles and continuous wheezes) while strictly bounding the computational complexity for endpoint deployments, we decouple the feature extraction pipeline into two divergent tracks: lightweight statistical features for the initial random forest layers, and hybrid spatial-spectral representations for the complex deep learning stage.

\subsubsection{Statistical Lightweight Features (Layers 1--3)}
We distill the acoustic signal into a highly compact, 43-dimensional tabular feature vector tailored precisely for rapid inference. This vector amalgamates crucial temporal and cepstral metrics to detect both transient anomalies and broad spectral deformations perfectly without the heavy operation overhead of forming images.

\textit{1) Time-Domain Transients:} We compute the Root Mean Square Energy (RMSE) and the Zero-Crossing Rate (ZCR) over the frame windows. ZCR is exceptionally potent for identifying the erratic, high-frequency spikes characteristic of crackles \cite{b20,b21}, whereas RMSE models the overarching inhalation and exhalation energy envelopes \cite{b19}. The mean and standard deviation of both metrics are aggregated across the signal duration to construct $4$ fundamental time-domain features. ZCR and RMSE are formally defined as:
\begin{equation}
ZCR = \frac{1}{2(N-1)} \sum_{n=1}^{N-1} \left| \text{sgn}[x(n)] - \text{sgn}[x(n-1)] \right|
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
RMSE = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} |x(n)|^2}
\end{equation}
where $N$ is the frame length and $x(n)$ denotes the discrete acoustic sample.

\textit{2) Mel-Frequency Cepstral Coefficients (MFCCs):} To encode the human-perceptual spectral envelope, we extract 13 MFCCs. The nonlinear perceived frequency spectrum is grounded in the Mel transformation scale:
\begin{equation}
m = 2595 \log_{10} \left( 1 + \frac{f}{700} \right)
\end{equation}
The global mean and standard deviation for each of the $13$ frequency bands are independently retained ($26$ dimensions). Furthermore, to encapsulate the dynamic temporal evolution across breathing cycles, the first-order derivatives ($\Delta$ MFCCs) are calculated, contributing an additional $13$ dimensions derived from their arithmetic means. In total, 39 cepstral features join the 4 temporal dimensions to form the definitive 43-feature signature vector.

\subsubsection{Hybrid Spectrogram Generation (Layer 4)}
For cases relegated to the Convolutional Neural Network (Layer 4), conventional approaches often rely on Continuous Wavelet Transforms (CWT) which incur prohibitive latency thresholds \cite{b27}. We supersede CWT with an algorithmic optimization utilizing a bespoke Fast Fourier Transform (FFT)-driven \textit{Hybrid Spectrogram}. This architecture extracts a dense, three-channel $224 \times 224$ matrix inherently harmonious with standard ImageNet-pretrained vision conduits. 

\textit{1) Channel 1 (Gammatonegram):} Mimics the cochlear filtering mechanisms of the human auditory system using an array of 64 Equivalent Rectangular Bandwidth (ERB) filters \cite{b28}. We expedite the creation of this filterbank by applying Gaussian-like Gammatone weights in the frequency domain, circumventing intensive time-domain convolutions. 

\textit{2) Channel 2 (Mel-Spectrogram):} Composed of 128 Mel-scale filters capturing the macroscopic static energy distributions, thereby providing steady anchors around fundamental physiological breath tones.

\textit{3) Channel 3 (Average Protocol):} Computes the arithmetic mean between the normalized Gammatone and Mel channels. This operation imposes a pervasive noise-smoothing regularizer across the frequency grid, artificially subduing errant audio artifacts.

Following logarithmic intensity compression, the respective spaces are synchronized using bilinear interpolation to enforce the critical $224 \times 224$ shape factor and scaled strictly to the $[0, 1]$ interval. This novel fusion circumvents CWT computational penalties.

\subsubsection{Feature Augmentation Protocol}
Due to the pervasive physical scarcity of exhaustive respiratory databases, empirical feature augmentation is integrated to synthetically multiply the acoustic variations during training. SpecAugment enforces persistent rectangular masks across localized time segments and frequency bins, impelling the neural layers to correlate disjointed acoustic textures. Concurrently, Vocal Tract Length Perturbation (VTLP) applies a probabilistic frequency warp parameter ($\alpha \in [0.9, 1.1]$). This non-linear distortion mirrors the varying anatomical dimensions of distinct respiratory trees observed across diverse patient demographics.

\subsection{Cascaded Early-Exit Classification Framework}
\label{subsec:architecture}
Standard deep learning models impose a uniform computational cost on all samples, regardless of difficulty. We propose a \textit{Cascaded Early-Exit Framework} that formulates diagnosis as a sequential decision process to optimize the accuracy-efficiency trade-off.

\subsubsection{Theoretical Formulation}
The system is structured into $L=4$ layers. Let $x$ be an input sample and $C_i$ be the computational cost (latency/energy) of layer $i$. The system terminates at layer $i$ if the confidence score $S_i(x)$ exceeds a learnable threshold $\tau_i$. The total expected inference cost $\mathbb{E}[Cost]$ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
\mathbb{E}[Cost] = C_1 + \sum_{i=2}^{L} C_i \cdot P(\text{exit} \ge i)
\end{equation}
where $P(\text{exit} \ge i)$ denotes the probability of the sample propagating to layer $i$. By placing low-cost Random Forest classifiers ($C_{RF}$) in the initial layers ($i=1,2,3$) and the high-cost CNN ($C_{CNN}$) only in the final layer ($i=4$), and ensuring $C_{RF} \ll C_{CNN}$, we minimize the total system energy while maintaining high accuracy for difficult samples.

\subsubsection{Hierarchical Architecture}
The framework implementation details are presented in Table \ref{tab:framework_config} and visualized in Figure \ref{fig2}.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Layers 1-3 (Statistical Screening via Random Forests):} These layers employ Random Forest (RF) ensembles to process the 43-dimensional tabular features (Time and Cepstral vectors). RFs are selected for the initial stages due to their low latency and absence of complex floating-point matrix multiplications. Each layer consists of 7 parallel RF ensembles (Septuple Forest), comprising distinct decision trees (Table \ref{tab:hyperparams}). A majority voting mechanism across these ensembles determines the early exit condition:
    \begin{equation}
    \text{Decision} = 
    \begin{cases} 
      \text{Class } c, & \text{if } \sum_{j=1}^{7} \mathbb{I}(O_j = c) \ge \lambda \\
      \text{Next Layer}, & \text{otherwise}
    \end{cases}
    \end{equation}
    where $O_j$ is the output of the $j$-th RF ensemble and $\lambda=4$ is the consensus threshold.
    
    \item \textbf{Layer 4 (Deep Pattern Recognition via Knowledge Distillation):} Samples with ambiguous statistical signatures (high uncertainty) propagate to the final layer. Here, the 3-channel Hybrid Spectrograms are processed by a highly optimized Convolutional Neural Network (CNN). To maximize diagnostic accuracy without violating the strict computational constraints of edge deployment, we employ a Knowledge Distillation (KD) training paradigm. A robust teacher ensemble comprising three EfficientNet-B0 architectures (trained via 4-fold cross-validation each, yielding 12 modeling components) is trained to capture complex acoustic representations. Subsequently, a lightweight student model, MobileNetV2 \cite{b35} with a custom classification head ($512 \rightarrow 256 \rightarrow 3$), is distilled to mimic the teacher's behavior. The student is supervised via a combined loss function integrating Focal Loss ($\gamma=2$, label smoothing=$0.1$) to handle class imbalance, and Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence with temperature scaling ($T=4, \alpha=0.7$) to align with the teacher ensemble's soft probabilities. This strategy allows the MobileNetV2 student, utilizing efficient Depthwise Separable Convolutions, to inherit the superior generalization of the heavy ensemble while maintaining minimal parameters and FLOPs suitable for hardware acceleration (Table \ref{tab:hyperparams}). During training, data is enhanced using SpecAugment and temporal audio augmentations.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Hardware-Aware Quantization Pipeline}
To bridge the gap between high-precision floating-point distillation and resource-constrained FPGA deployment, a Quantization-Aware Training (QAT) pipeline is applied to the MobileNetV2 student model. Following post-training calibration (PTQ) to determine optimal tensor scaling factors using a Max calibrator over 32 batches, the model undergoes QAT fine-tuning for 5 epochs with a conservative learning rate of $1e^{-5}$. The resulting INT8 model achieves a remarkable Signal-to-Quantization-Noise Ratio (SQNR) of -4.37 dB and is exported via ONNX with \texttt{QuantizeLinear} and \texttt{DequantizeLinear} (QDQ) nodes at opset 13. This rigorous quantization preserves diagnostic accuracy while dramatically reducing inference latency and securing compliance with integer-only hardware accelerators like the DPU.

\subsection{Heterogeneous Hardware Implementation}
The proposed architecture is synthesized on the Xilinx Zynq UltraScale+ MPSoC (Ultra96-V2) using a Hardware-Software Co-design approach to maximize throughput.

\textit{1) Programmable Logic (PL) Acceleration:} The Random Forest ensembles are mapped directly to the FPGA fabric using custom HDL IP cores. The decision trees are unrolled to exploit fine-grained parallelism, allowing the evaluation of multiple trees within a single clock cycle, significantly reducing the latency of the first three layers.

\textit{2) DPU Integration:} The CNN inference is accelerated using the Xilinx Deep Learning Processor Unit (DPU). The DPU is configured to execute tensor operations efficiently, utilizing the DSP48 slices for matrix multiplications and on-chip Block RAM (BRAM) for weight caching. 

This heterogeneous mapping ensures that "easy" samples are processed almost instantaneously by the PL logic, while "hard" samples receive the full computational power of the DPU, realizing the theoretical energy savings of the cascaded model.
