\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:relatedwork}

The development of automated respiratory pathology classification has evolved from classical machine learning paradigms toward deep representational learning. This section reviews prior studies categorized by their algorithmic contributions, IoT integration, and hardware acceleration strategies.

\subsection{Deep Learning in Respiratory Sound Classification}
The benchmark for automated lung sound analysis was significantly advanced by the ICBHI 2017 challenge, leading to a transition from handcrafted acoustic features to deep neural architectures. Noureddine et al. \cite{noureddine2020} demonstrated that Convolutional Recurrent Neural Networks (CRNNs) can effectively capture both the spectral characteristics and temporal dynamics of adventitious sounds, such as crackles and wheezes. 

Recent state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods have further improved diagnostic accuracy through specialized feature extraction and attention mechanisms. Ariyanti et al. \cite{b07} proposed an Audio-Spectrogram Vision Transformer (AS-ViT) to leverage self-attention for pattern recognition, achieving 86.4\% accuracy. Other studies have explored multi-task learning (MTL) approaches for simultaneous sound and disease classification \cite{b15}, and improved residual networks for stable gradient flow \cite{b09}. While these models achieve high diagnostic performance, their computational complexity poses a significant challenge for real-time deployment on resource-constrained edge devices. \subsection{Edge-IoT Ecosystems for Healthcare Monitoring}
The shift toward Edge-IoT computing in healthcare is driven by the need for low-latency response and privacy-preserving data handling. Gu et al. \cite{gu2024mauth} highlighted the potential of non-contact respiratory monitoring within the IEEE IoT ecosystem, emphasizing the importance of local processing to eliminate the round-trip latency of cloud-based diagnosis. Edge-centric frameworks have been shown to reduce patient evaluation time by providing immediate triage and reducing diagnostic latency by up to 68\%. 

Despite these benefits, current IoT-based respiratory diagnostic systems primarily focus on data transmission or static classification. Most existing pipelines are evaluated as static offline classifiers where every sample is processed by the same model regardless of its diagnostic difficulty. This motivates the need for uncertainty-aware architectures that can adapt their computational intensity to the clinical complexity of each input sample.

\subsection{FPGA Acceleration and Hardware-Software Co-design}
Field-Programmable Gate Arrays (FPGAs) have emerged as a dominant platform for medical AI acceleration due to their deterministic latency and superior energy-per-inference compared to mobile GPUs. The deployment of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) on FPGAs has been streamlined by the Xilinx Deep Learning Processor Unit (DPU) and the Vitis AI toolchain. Recent studies have successfully accelerated GAN-based medical image reconstruction \cite{zhu2023} and real-time audio classification \cite{fazilat2023} using DPU overlays. 

Nevertheless, existing FPGA implementations typically follow a static execution model where the entire network is activated for every input. Furthermore, while studies such as \cite{b13} and \cite{b14} have optimized model size for embedded healthcare, they often do not fully exploit the parallel computing power and flexibility of FPGAs for uncertainty-aware inference. Our proposed UA-HCI framework addresses this gap by combining the Processing System (PS)-side lightweight cascaded screening with Programmable Logic (PL)-side DPU acceleration, ensuring that the high-power programmable logic is reserved only for clinically uncertain samples.
