\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:relatedwork}
Extensive research efforts have been directed toward the development of respiratory pathology classification methodologies, predominantly employing post-segmentation approaches with classical machine learning paradigms, including Support Vector Machines (SVM), Artificial Neural Network architectures, K-Nearest Neighbor (KNN) algorithms, Multilayer Perceptron (MLP) frameworks, Random Forest (RF) ensembles, and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks. Table~\ref{tab:ml_comparison} presents a comprehensive synthesis of significant contributions in this domain, cataloging investigations that leverage computational intelligence techniques for pulmonary disorder diagnosis across diverse respiratory conditions.
The predominant methodological frameworks in this research domain employ conventional taxonomic techniques that leverage characteristic feature representations as their foundational classification mechanism. The substantive differentiation among these methodological approaches, however, manifests primarily in their diverse feature extraction algorithms—each designed to optimize the identification and selection of classification methodologies with maximal diagnostic efficacy and computational performance.

Ariyanti \textit{et al.} \cite{b07} proposed an Audio-Spectrogram Vision Transformer (AS-ViT) for classifying respiratory sounds. The model converts lung sounds into spectrogram images using Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT) and applies a vision transformer to analyze them. The method, evaluated on the ICBHI 2017 dataset, achieved 86.4\% accuracy. Compared to previous methods, AS-ViT improved feature extraction by leveraging self-attention mechanisms, making it more effective in recognizing different respiratory sound patterns.

Raju and Ramesh \cite{b08} introduced a deep learning framework optimized for respiratory disease classification using COPD audio data from Kaggle. Their model extracts Mel-Frequency Cepstral Coefficients (MFCC) and spectrogram features, achieving 93\% accuracy. By combining these features with a convolutional neural network (CNN), the model enhances classification performance. This work highlights the potential of non-invasive respiratory sound analysis in early disease detection, particularly for conditions such as COPD and pneumonia.

Wu \textit{et al.} \cite{b09} developed an improved Bi-ResNet model for lung sound classification. The model integrates STFT and wavelet transform features, enhancing feature extraction efficiency. By incorporating skip connections and direct feature integration, the model achieves a more stable gradient flow, reducing vanishing gradient issues. Experimental results on the ICBHI 2017 dataset demonstrated a classification accuracy of 77.81\%, a 25.02\% improvement over previous Bi-ResNet implementations.

Edakkadan and Srivastava \cite{b10} proposed a portable respiratory sound classification system using CNNs. Their method converts lung sounds into Mel spectrograms and classifies them using a lightweight CNN architecture. By implementing the system on a Raspberry Pi, the authors demonstrated its feasibility for real-time classification in mobile healthcare applications. The model achieved an accuracy of 80.55\% on the HF Lung dataset, with a sensitivity of 95.65\% and specificity of 98.80\%.

Widyagustin \textit{et al.} \cite{b11} applied YAMNet for lung sound classification, leveraging transfer learning to extract features from respiratory audio. The model was trained on a dataset consisting of 1132 samples across five lung sound categories, achieving an accuracy of 85.47\%. YAMNet's ability to utilize pre-trained audio embeddings improved classification performance, making it an efficient approach for automated lung disease identification.

Latifi \textit{et al.} \cite{b12} introduced novel feature extraction techniques, Maximum Gabor Filter Bank Entropy (MAGFB) and Maximum Mel Filter Bank Entropy (MAMFB), for lung sound classification. When tested on the ICBHI 2017 dataset, their method outperformed traditional MFCC and wavelet-based techniques, achieving a classification accuracy of 93\%. The proposed approach demonstrated that entropy-based feature extraction methods could significantly enhance the representation of respiratory sound patterns.

Roy and Satija \cite{b13} developed RDLINet, a lightweight inception network designed for multi-class respiratory disease classification. The model processes Mel spectrograms and classifies lung diseases with an accuracy of 99.6\% in six-class classification. By reducing model complexity and improving inference speed, RDLINet is suitable for deployment in resource-constrained environments such as embedded healthcare devices.

Subsequently, Roy and Satija \cite{b14} proposed ILDNet, a deep learning framework for interstitial lung disease (ILD) identification. The model employs sinc convolution-based residual networks to extract complex respiratory sound features. Validated on BRACETS and KAUH datasets, the experimental results showed an accuracy of 81.25\%. The study emphasizes the importance of using advanced convolutional architectures for the early detection of ILD.

K.V \textit{et al.} \cite{b15} introduced a multi-task learning (MTL) approach for the simultaneous classification of lung sounds and diseases. Their model integrates CNN, ResNet50, MobileNet, and DenseNet architectures, achieving 74\% accuracy for lung sound classification and 91\% for disease classification. Additionally, the authors implemented a risk level computation for COPD using demographic data, where the Random Forest classifier achieved the highest accuracy of 92\%. This work underscores the potential of MTL in improving diagnostic precision and clinical decision-making.

However, it is worth noting that most studies focus on maximizing the accuracy of classification results without fully exploiting the parallel computing power and flexibility of FPGAs in medical data processing, particularly in respiratory disease diagnosis. 
Auscultation of pulmonary sounds via stethoscope continues to be among the most essential and practical diagnostic approaches for identifying respiratory conditions in patients. Nevertheless, the acoustic patterns produced by the respiratory system exhibit considerable complexity and unpredictability, presenting interpretive challenges even for healthcare professionals with specialized training.
This creates a gap in applying the advantages of FPGA architecture to the medical field, where accurate and rapid diagnosis is crucial. The current state of affairs motivates the need for research and application of deep learning models on FPGAs to improve the diagnosis and management of respiratory diseases, mainly through lung sounds.
