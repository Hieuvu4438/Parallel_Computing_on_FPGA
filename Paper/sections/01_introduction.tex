\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
\IEEEPARstart{R}{espiratory} diseases remain a major public-health challenge, and chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) is among the most burdensome conditions because of its high prevalence, progressive airflow limitation, and recurrent exacerbations~\cite{b01}. COPD contributes substantially to mortality and long-term disability worldwide~\cite{b02,b03}, while underdiagnosis remains common in routine practice. One practical reason is that auscultation findings are often subtle, variable across examiners, and difficult to standardize, especially outside tertiary-care settings.

Automated respiratory sound analysis therefore has strong potential as a non-invasive screening support tool. Prior work has shown that machine learning and deep learning can extract clinically relevant acoustic patterns from lung sounds, including crackles and wheezes, using spectrogram-based convolutional models, recurrent models, and transformer-style encoders~\cite{b07,b08,b09,b10,b11,b12,b13,b14,b15}. These advances have improved classification performance, but most reported pipelines still follow a \emph{static inference} paradigm: every sample is processed by a deep model regardless of sample difficulty.

For edge deployment, static inference is often suboptimal. Portable and embedded systems must satisfy latency, memory, and power constraints while preserving sufficient diagnostic sensitivity. In this context, not all respiratory segments require the same computational budget: easy samples can often be resolved by lightweight descriptors, whereas uncertain samples benefit from richer time--frequency representations and deep inference. This motivates uncertainty-aware routing strategies that adapt computation to input complexity instead of applying uniform heavy inference.

In parallel, FPGA-based heterogeneous platforms have become increasingly attractive for medical edge AI because they combine programmable logic acceleration with embedded CPUs in a single device. Existing studies report effective FPGA acceleration for deep models in healthcare and signal-processing scenarios~\cite{zhu2023,fazilat2023}, but respiratory sound pipelines on embedded hardware are still predominantly evaluated from an accuracy-centric perspective. Joint optimization of \emph{diagnostic quality}, \emph{uncertainty-aware routing}, and \emph{hardware--software partitioning} remains relatively underexplored.

To address this gap, we propose an \textit{Uncertainty-Aware Heterogeneous Cascaded Inference} (UA-HCI) framework for respiratory sound analysis on FPGA-based edge devices. The framework uses lightweight one-dimensional acoustic statistics with Random Forest screening layers on the ARM Processing System (PS), and routes only uncertain samples to a MobileNetV2-based INT8 CNN path on the Programmable Logic (PL) via the Xilinx DPU. This design aims to reduce unnecessary deep inference while retaining strong classification performance.

The main contributions of this work are as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We introduce UA-HCI, a four-layer cascaded inference architecture that combines uncertainty-aware early exits with deep classification for difficult samples.
    \item We implement a heterogeneous PS--PL deployment strategy in which control-dominant lightweight stages run on the embedded ARM processor, while compute-intensive CNN inference is offloaded to the FPGA DPU.
    \item We evaluate the framework on a merged ICBHI+KAUH respiratory sound corpus under subject-independent splitting, and report both classification performance and hardware-oriented measurement protocols for route-level latency and energy analysis.
\end{itemize}
